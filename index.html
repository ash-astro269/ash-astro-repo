Bigger, brighter moon.

Wolf Moon

The Wolf Moon is the traditional name for the first full moon of the calendar year, which typically occurs in January.

Tuesday, November 24

Beaver Moon

The Beaver Moon is the traditional name for the full moon that occurs in November, marking the time when beavers are actively preparing their lodges for winter.

Wednesday, December 23

Cold Moon

The Cold Moon is the traditional name for the December full moon, which signifies the arrival of winter's deep freeze and the year's longest nights.

Eclipses in 2026

Celestial alignment shadow event & temporary obscuring of light.

February 17

Annular Solar Eclipse

An annular solar eclipse, or "ring of fire" eclipse, happens when the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth, but is too far away to completely block the Sun, leaving a bright, fiery ring of sunlight visible around the Moon's silhouette.

March 2-3

Total Lunar Eclipse

A total lunar eclipse is when Earth perfectly aligns between the Sun and Moon, completely blocking direct sunlight and casting the Moon into Earth's dark shadow (umbra), often turning it a stunning red or orange hue as sunlight bends through our atmosphere.

August 12

Total Solar Eclipse

A total solar eclipse is when the Moon perfectly aligns between the Sun and Earth, completely blocking the Sun's bright face and casting a temporary, eerie darkness over a small area, allowing the Sun's faint outer atmosphere (corona) to become visible.

August 27-28

Partial Lunar Eclipse

A partial lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth passes between the Sun and Moon, but not in perfect alignment, so only a portion of the Moon enters Earth's dark shadow (umbra), creating a dark, curved bite on the lunar surface while the rest remains lit by the Sun.

Meteor Showers in 2026

Nightly shooting stars.

January 2-3

Quadrantid Meteor Shower

Quadrantids are known for their short, intense peak. They are best seen in the Northern Hemisphere.

April 21-22

Lyrid Meteor Shower

The Lyrids are usually a moderate meteor shower, best viewed from the Northern Hemisphere.

May 5-6

Eta Aquariid Meteor Shower

The Eta Aquariids are best seen from the Southern Hemisphere and can be prolific, producing fast and bright meteors.

July 30-31

Southern Delta Aquariid Meteor Shower

The Southern Delta Aquariids, associated with comet 96P/Machholz, can be seen from the Southern Hemisphere. Their fast, faint meteors radiate from the constellation Aquarius.

July 30-31

Alpha Capricornid Meteor Shower

The Alpha Capricornids meteor shower produces a relatively small number of meteors but is notable for its bright, slow-moving fireballs. They can be seen in the Northern and Southern hemispheres.

August 12-13

Perseid Meteor Shower

The Perseids are one of the most popular meteor showers of the year, offering a good show for viewers in the Northern Hemisphere. They are known for their bright meteors.

October 21-22

Orionid Meteor Shower

The Orionids are best viewed from the Northern Hemisphere and are known for their fast-moving meteors associated with Halley's Comet.

November 4-5

Taurid Meteor Shower

The Taurids are known for their long duration and relatively low meteor rate. They are visible in the Southern and Northern hemispheres.

November 17

Leonid Meteor Shower

The Leonids have produced some of the most impressive meteor storms in history. While such storms are infrequent, the Leonids are worth watching and can be seen in both hemispheres.

December 13-14

Geminid Meteor Shower

The Geminids are one of the most reliable and active meteor showers of the year, producing a high number of bright meteors. The Geminid meteor shower is most prominent in the Northern Hemisphere, but it can also be seen in the Southern Hemisphere.

December 22

Ursid Meteor Shower

The Ursids are associated with the comet 8P/Tuttle and the shower's radiant point appears to originate from the constellation Ursa Minor (the Little Dipper). The Ursid meteor shower is visible in the Northern Hemisphere.
